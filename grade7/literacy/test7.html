READING COMPREHENSION

When Sipho came to my class, he was nearly seven years old. Within two weeks of starting school, he was already proving to be difficult in class. He was playful, often teased other children and disturbed them in class. He refused to write anything and always lost his pencils.

One day, he was sitting on the carpet with the rest of the class. I asked some children in the class to tell me their news. Sipho wanted to talk but I did not let him have a turn that day. At the end of the session, everybody had to go back to their desks and draw a picture of some news. Sipho refused to return to his desk.

When I repeated my instruction, he pretended that he did not understand me. When I went over to him to lead him back to his seat, he ran away, racing through the class laughing at me. I got very angry and told him to be back in his seat by the count of three. I counted to three but he was still running around laughing.

Although I was angry, I did not want to hit him. However, I felt degraded at the thought of chasing Sipho in front of all other children. All these feelings made me react in a personal way. The next time Sipho came close to me, I grabbed him and pushed him out of the classroom. Once Sipho was outside, I closed the door. He made a lot of noise such that everybody in the school knew what was going on.

Eventually, the headteacher came around and asked what had happened. She then took Sipho to her office and spoke to him, trying to find out why he was so troublesome, In the end, Sipho had to apologise to me before he was allowed back into my class. I was so grateful that the headteacher was so supportive, but deep inside I felt like a failure.
